Your Summer at Towanda: What to Expect & How to Succeed
Welcome

Welcome to the Towanda Family.

Whether you're coming from down the road or across the world, it's completely normal to feel excited, nervous, curious, or overwhelmed as you get ready for your first summer at Camp Towanda. You might be arriving not knowing anyone yet. You may not be entirely sure what a camp counselor truly does day to day. You could be wondering if you'll fit in, make close friends, handle the responsibility, or feel confident in your role. All of that is normal.

Take a deep breath. This guide is designed to reduce anxiety, clarify expectations, and help you understand what your summer will truly look like. It shows how everything fits together: your schedule, your responsibilities, your boundaries, your growth, and your role in the Towanda community.

Our Orientation is intentionally planned to help you build friendships, understand the culture of camp, learn your job expectations, and meet the people you can rely on for guidance and support all summer long.

You do not need to be perfect to be successful at camp. You need to be present, responsible, and open to growth.

You've got this, and we've got you.

The Camp Experience
1.1 What Camp Actually Feels Like
Section 1

What Camp Actually Feels Like

Here is an honest look at what camp truly feels like so you can arrive prepared:

Camp is intense. Every day is full, and there is rarely a quiet moment. That constant movement and energy are part of what makes it feel so alive. Your days will be structured, with the schedule driving everything you do. That structure keeps campers safe and helps make even the busiest days manageable.

You will live, eat, work, and grow alongside your fellow counselors. Camp is not a quiet place. It is loud, active, and full of energy. Lean in and be part of it. From the moment you wake up until lights out, you will be on. It is exhausting at times, but it is a different kind of tired, the kind that comes from doing something that matters. Through it all, your impact on your campers is real and lasting. You may not always see it in the moment, but it is there.

Section 1

Feelings to Expect

There are also feelings you should expect along the way. Many first-year counselors experience them. Being prepared can go a long way toward helping you be successful this summer.

You may feel homesick. Yes, staff get homesick too. It usually passes within the first week once you are busy and begin to feel connected.
You may feel overwhelmed in week one. There is a lot to absorb. Give yourself grace. It will start to click sooner than you think.
You will adjust to living in close quarters with your campers. Missing privacy is real, but it is also part of what makes the friendships so strong. Some days will bring emotional highs and lows. You may feel proud, frustrated, grateful, and tired, sometimes all in the same afternoon. That is camp.
Along the way, you will discover unexpected confidence. You will handle situations you did not think you could. You will problem solve, lead, and grow faster than you expect. You will build deep connections with other staff that feel real and lasting, because shared challenge creates strong bonds.
There will be moments of joy that catch you off guard, moments when you look around and think, I cannot believe I get to do this for a job. You will feel pride in your campers as you watch them try something new, push through fear, or make a friend. Those moments will stay with you long after the summer ends.
At the end of a long day, you may be exhausted, but you will know your work mattered.
You don't need to be perfect. You need to be present, responsible, and open to growth. You've got this, and we've got you.

What You're Probably Wondering About
Section 2 — 2.1

Will I Make Friends?

Yes. Without a doubt, yes!

Living in close quarters with people who are going through the same experience accelerates connection in a way that everyday life rarely does. You do not have to force it. You just have to show up consistently and be part of it. Along the way, you will build friendships rooted in shared experiences that often make no sense to the people at home. You will become part of a community with inside jokes, stories, and moments that do not fully translate to the outside world. If you are open to it, these can become lifelong friendships.

It also helps to know a few things going in.

People arrive from over 20 U.S. states and 20 countries. The diversity within your camp community is one of its greatest strengths. You will meet people you likely would never have crossed paths with anywhere else, and that exposure will broaden your perspective in meaningful ways.
Conflict will happen. Any time you bring people together in close quarters with high responsibility and limited sleep, friction is inevitable. That is not a sign that something is wrong. It is part of living and working closely with others. What matters most is how you handle those moments.
You do not have to like everyone. You do have to treat everyone with professionalism and respect. That is the standard, and it applies to everyone.
Keep in mind that culture shock is real, especially for international staff. Give yourself time to adjust, and extend that same patience and understanding to others who are adjusting as well.
Section 2 — 2.2

How Much Free Time Will I Actually Have?

This is one of the most common questions, and you deserve a straight answer.

Periods Off

Camp makes every effort to provide each counselor with a period off during the activity day from time to time. It cannot be guaranteed every day and depends on coverage. If you are scheduled for a period off, you are expected to be with your group for the first and last five minutes of that period. That is the expectation.

Nights Off

You can expect roughly 2 to 3 nights off per week. A night off begins once your bunk is officially covered and the announcement is made from the H.C. Shack. You must sign yourself out and back in. No one can sign in for you.

Curfew

On most nights, curfew is midnight. Earlier curfews may be announced by the Director or Head Counselors when necessary. This is not a rule for the sake of having a rule. It is about making sure you are rested and fully functional the next morning for the campers who depend on you. If you miss curfew and are not signed back in, staff will begin looking for you. That process is serious and avoidable.

Section 2 — 2.2 continued

How Much Free Time Will I Actually Have?

Days Off

Over the 8 week season, you will receive approximately 1 day off a week. One of those days can be extended into a 30 hour overnight if you would like to get further away and fully recharge.

What "Off Duty" Actually Means

When you are off duty, you can go into town, relax, and be yourself. At the same time, you are still representing Towanda wherever you go. Be courteous, drive carefully, and carry yourself in a way that reflects well on you and on camp.

On-Camp Nights

Some nights off are on-campus only. Do not be disappointed by that. These nights are often some of the most enjoyable. The Swamp is our staff nightclub, and everything there is free for you to enjoy. You also have access to the Staff Lounge, the White Rock Cafe, volleyball, basketball, campfires, and swimming. Counselors who choose to stay on camp for their nights off often end up having the best experiences.

Time off is scheduled and earned. It depends on campers being responsibly covered. We plan it carefully and take it seriously.
Section 2 — 2.3

Where Will I Sleep?

General counselors live in the bunks with their campers.
Senior and Key Staff may be assigned to designated senior housing.
Specialty counselors also live in bunks alongside their campers and carry the same bunk responsibilities as all other counselors.

Most beds are bunk beds. If you would like a railing or ladder for the top bunk, let us know when you arrive. If you do not request one, we will assume you do not need it.

Section 2 — 2.4

What About My Phone?

Camp Towanda runs as tech-free, social-media-free, and online-free as possible. This isn't arbitrary — it's one of the things that makes camp feel like camp. When phones are in the room, presence isn't. And presence is everything here.

Your phone goes into a personal cell locker during all on-duty hours.
You can access your locker on your nights and days off.
No phones inside the bunk or within sight of campers, guests, or on-duty staff.
No photography inside bunks.
No video streaming or downloading on camp Wi-Fi.
Every new staff member gets a mobile battery charger from camp.
Give your family the camp office number for emergencies: 570-253-3266. Let your friends know you'll check voicemail on your scheduled nights and days off. They'll adjust.
Section 2 — 2.5

What About Wi-Fi and Email?

Wi-Fi is available in the Staff Lounge and White Rock Cafe, for off-duty staff only. It's a rural internet provider, so it's not always fast or perfectly reliable. We do our best to keep it running. Acceptable use includes email, simple browsing, online classes, work requirements, and bill payments. Streaming and downloading aren't permitted.

If you need faster Wi-Fi, McDonald's, Walmart, Dunkin' Donuts, and the local library (open weekdays 10am–6pm) all offer free access.

Section 3
The Job, Honestly
Section 3 — 3.1

The Role You Play in Your Campers' Lives

Some people arrive at camp thinking the job is mostly about running activities. In reality, it is much more than that. On any given day, you are taking on multiple roles at once.

You are a caregiver, attending to the physical and emotional needs of children who are far from home.
You are a role model. Everything you say and do is being observed and absorbed by the campers around you.
You are a safety supervisor. Your attentiveness and presence serve as the first line of protection.
You are an emotional anchor. Homesickness, conflict, fear, and frustration often come to you first.
You are an energy leader. Your attitude truly sets the tone for the entire group, every single day.
Camper safety is your #1 responsibility at all times. Not an activity. Not a relationship. Safety.
Section 3 — 3.2

What We Expect from You: Available

At its core, here is what we are asking you to be: Available, Responsible, and Adaptable.

Available
Be fully present. Bring consistent positive energy and emotional investment into every space you enter. Your campers will mirror what you bring.
Approach every activity with enthusiasm, even the ones that may not seem exciting to you. If you treat a rainy practice, a schedule change, or a quieter moment like it still matters, your campers will respond to that energy and engage with it too.
Engage physically and mentally. Stand with your campers. Sit with them at meals. Participate when appropriate. Supervise actively, not from the sidelines.
Be attentive and safety minded. Notice what is happening around you. If something feels off, speak up.
Protect camper dignity. Address concerns respectfully and privately whenever possible. Build kids up.
Put your phone away when you are with campers and during responsibilities. Presence matters.
After a hard moment, circle back. Repair connection. Stay emotionally invested.
Section 3 — 3.2 continued

What We Expect from You: Responsible

Responsible
Be punctual and prepared. Arrive on time and ready. Your consistency keeps the day running smoothly.
Use professional language at all times.
Treat campers and fellow staff with respect. Speak in ways that reflect maturity and care.
Keep complaints and frustrations away from campers. Maintain a positive and stable environment for them.
Contribute to a culture of trust by staying out of gossip.
Follow through on your commitments. Your campers are depending on you.
Take shared responsibility for safety, culture, and standards. Hold yourself to them.
Section 3 — 3.2 continued

What We Expect from You: Adaptable

Adaptable
Be flexible when the schedule changes. It will change. Adjust and move forward with a steady mindset.
Regulate your emotions before helping others regulate theirs. Self awareness is leadership.
Take ownership of mistakes early. Address them directly and learn from them.
Ask for help before a small issue becomes a larger one. Seeking support is strength.
Be open to feedback and willing to grow. Camp is demanding, and growth happens quickly when you choose it.
Section 3 — 3.3

Who Do I Go to When I Need Help?

You are not figuring this out alone. There are people here whose entire role is to support you, and they take that responsibility seriously.

Your Head Counselors (H.C.s) should be your first stop for anything involving your campers, your bunk, or your responsibilities. They would much rather hear from you early, before something grows into a bigger issue.
Your Group Leaders (G.L.s) live with campers and are nearby throughout the day. They are in it with you and understand exactly what your day looks like.
Camp Moms are exactly what the name suggests. They are here for everyone. If you are struggling and are not sure where to turn, start with them.
The Directors, Mendy and Alana, are accessible. Even when they appear busy, they want to hear from you.
The Health Center is open 24 hours a day for emergencies. Our medical team, including doctors, nurses, and an EMT, are on campus and ready to respond.
Asking for help is a sign of maturity here, not weakness. It's what good counselors do.
Section 3 — 3.4

Adjusting to Life at Camp

There are a few expectations that will not serve you here. The sooner you let them go, the smoother your summer will feel.

Privacy — You will be living with your campers, and alone time is limited. Plan for it when you can and appreciate it when it happens.
Constant Praise — You will do meaningful, difficult work that may not always be formally recognized. Do it anyway. The impact matters, even when it is quiet. It is always appreciated.
Unlimited Phone Access — When you are on duty, your phone lives in your locker.
Negotiating Rules — The rules at camp were built over years of real experience. Trust that they exist for a reason.
Putting Peer Relationship above your Responsibilities — Your personal life comes second while you are on duty.
Section 3 — 3.5

Boundaries: And Why They Protect Everyone

Camp is a children's community. We talk about boundaries at camp not because we don't trust you, but because clear expectations protect you, your campers, and their families. When boundaries are unclear, everyone suffers. When they're clear, everyone thrives.

You can be warm and caring with your campers. You can genuinely love this job. And you are still the adult in the relationship. That distinction matters.

Here's what professional boundaries look like at Towanda:

Section 3 — Boundaries

Rule of Three

You are never alone with a single camper in any building, outdoor space, vehicle, or enclosed area.

Even when speaking individually with a camper, remain within sight of other adults.

Walk and talks must stay visible.
Doors remain open.
Conversations happen in shared or observable spaces.
If a situation starts to become one on one, correct it immediately. Move to a visible space. Bring another camper or staff member into the area. There are no exceptions to this rule.
Section 3 — Boundaries

No Secrets

There are no secrets between a counselor and a camper.

You may not:
Promise confidentiality
Ask a child to keep a secret
Say, "This stays between us"
Agree to keep information from leadership
If a camper begins to share something serious, respond honestly: "I care about you, and if this affects your safety, I will need to tell someone who can help." Reporting is not a betrayal of trust. It is part of your responsibility.
Section 3 — Boundaries

No Sexualized Talk or Behavior

Zero tolerance.
This includes but is not limited to:
Sexual jokes or innuendo
Conversations about your dating or sexual life
Sharing or referencing sexual content
Flirtation of any kind

You are the adult in the relationship. Always. If you are unsure whether something crosses a line, do not say it.

Section 3 — Boundaries

No Private Digital Communication

All communication with campers happens in person and in the open.

You may not:
Direct message, text, Snapchat, or email campers
Communicate with campers through personal social media accounts
Initiate post summer contact without written authorization from both the Director and the camper's parent or guardian
If accidental communication occurs, notify leadership immediately and copy the appropriate supervisor. Digital boundaries are as serious as physical boundaries.
Section 3 — Boundaries

Mandatory Reporting

If you suspect, observe, or hear about abuse, grooming behavior, or inappropriate conduct, report it immediately to the Director or a Head Counselor.

You do not:
Investigate
Interview
Confront the individual
Decide whether it is serious enough
If it concerns you, report it. It is far better to report and be wrong than to keep it to yourself and put a child at risk. Failure to report is grounds for immediate dismissal. Good faith reports are protected. Retaliation is not tolerated.
Section 3 — Boundaries

Appropriate Physical Contact Only

Physical contact must always be brief, appropriate, and in the open.

Acceptable:
A side hug
A high five
A brief supportive hand on the shoulder
Not acceptable:
Extended embraces
Lap sitting
Isolated physical contact
Anything that could reasonably be misinterpreted
When in doubt, create space. If you would not be comfortable with a parent observing the interaction, do not do it.
Clear boundaries create safety. Clear boundaries protect relationships. Clear boundaries allow everyone to do their job with confidence.

Section 4 — 4.1

When Do I Show Up?

Orientation begins at 9:00 AM Eastern Daylight Time. Plan to arrive the day before your orientation start date. If your training starts June 5th, you should arrive June 4th.

When you pull in, the very first thing that happens is a head lice inspection by our nursing staff. This isn't optional and it applies to everyone — new and returning.
Section 4 — 4.2

What Should I Bring?

Bring These:
All completed forms and documents through CampMinder and Campanion. Due May 1st.
Two government-issued forms of ID, required for the I-9.
Your health insurance card. Upload it to CampMinder.
Medications in their original packaging with dosage instructions.
A school pennant and hometown brochures for The Canteen.
An MP3 player without internet access if you would like personal music.
Practical, comfortable clothing and sturdy footwear.
Plenty of sunscreen.
Camp will give you a personal cell phone locker with your own combination. Your phone lives there during on-duty hours.

Platform shoes or heels. Safety hazard.

Section 5
Money & Pay
Section 5 — 5.1

When Do I Get Paid?

Your compensation is paid in four equal installments: June 30, July 15, July 31, and the last day of camp. Your final payment goes out after evaluations and surveys are submitted, bunks are cleaned, and all campers have made it home safely.

5.2 A Word About Your Contract

Your contract is private and non-negotiable. If anything feels unclear, address it before you arrive — not once you're here. When you complete the full season, you receive your full compensation minus any outstanding expenses. Violating the terms of your contract makes it subject to termination.

Section 5 — 5.3

Cash, Canteen, and Gratuities

We'd recommend not keeping large amounts of cash in your bunk. Camp has a low-fee ATM on site. The Canteen — our candy, ice cream, and snack bar — is available when your group is scheduled.

No tips or gratuities are accepted here. That's an ACA standard, and camp families already know about it.
Section 5 — 5.4

Taxes

Before you arrive, complete a W-4 and I-9 through CampMinder/Campanion. You'll need two government-issued forms of ID for the I-9. Your W-2 will come in January after the summer.

For First-Year International Staff

If this is your first time working in the U.S., camp will help you get your Social Security card after you arrive. Once you have your number, you'll need to share it with us — it's required by U.S. law.

Section 5 — 5.5 & 5.6

Insurance & After Camp

5.5 Insurance

Worker's Compensation covers any injuries that happen while you're doing your job. Camp is not responsible for injuries from personal activities that aren't part of camper supervision. You need to carry your own medical insurance — upload your carrier info to your Health History form. If you don't have coverage, email [email protected] before you arrive and we'll help you figure it out. International staff should check with their sponsoring agency.

5.6 After Camp

Work is available after your contract ends through September — light, flexible, and with additional pay. Email [email protected] if you're interested. If you'd like a letter of recommendation for your portfolio, we're happy to write one — just give us enough lead time before your deadline.

Section 6
Food, Sleep & Taking Care of Yourself
Section 6 — 6.1 & 6.2

What's the Food Like?

Meals are served family style by our LITs. Camp runs a kosher-style kitchen — that means no pork products and no dairy served with meat. The salad bar is available at every meal and does include dairy items like cheese. Peanut butter, jelly, and bread are on the table every meal. Coffee is available daily.

Desserts show up regularly. Ice cream sundaes are a camp-favorite event. Milk & Cookies is a nightly ritual on each campus. Fruit Call happens at the end of Rest Hour. Pretzels and granola bars are always available in the H.C. Shack. You will not go hungry.

6.2 What If I Have Dietary Restrictions?

Include any dietary restrictions, food allergies, or special needs in your Online Health History form. We have a camp nutritionist who advises on all menus, and we want to be ready for you before you arrive — not scrambling after you get here. Flag it now.

Camp is nut-aware, not nut-free. Peanut butter is available at meals at a designated separate table.
Section 6 — 6.3

What If I Get Sick?

Our Infirmary is staffed by doctors, nurses, and an EMT and operates similarly to a children's hospital. It's open 24 hours for emergencies. Daily medications are distributed at mealtimes — we call it Daily Vitamin Call to keep it private. Sick visits happen after breakfast and dinner (Health Call). If you're not well, go. Don't tough it out — it rarely helps and often spreads.

One firm rule: don't treat yourself, and don't treat your campers. Never give a camper any medication, cream, or ointment. Report every injury or symptom to the Health Center, no matter how minor it seems.
Section 6 — 6.4

Sleep — Take It Seriously

This isn't a lecture. It's practical advice from everyone who's done this job before you: sleep matters more here than almost anywhere else. Most people need 7.5 to 9 hours to perform well. When you're sleep-deprived, you're slower, shorter-tempered, less patient, and less present — all things your campers feel directly.

Use your nights off to rest when you can. The quality of who you are during the day is directly tied to how much you slept the night before.

Section 6 — 6.5

Will This Actually Be Worth It?

Yes. Without qualification, yes.

Every summer, former staff tell us that camp was one of the most formative experiences of their lives. Not just personally — professionally too. Here's what this experience can give you if you show up fully:

Real leadership skills — communication, crisis management, emotional intelligence, cross-cultural fluency.
Resume value that stands out — eight weeks of 24/7 responsibility for the safety and development of children is not something most 20-year-olds can claim.
Friendships that last — with people from around the world.
Self-knowledge — you'll learn more about who you are here than in almost any other environment.
Perspective — stepping away from screens, news, and your normal routine and into full presence forces real growth.
You are not just working a summer job. You are changing children's lives. And quietly, you might be changing your own.
You've got this, and we've got you.
